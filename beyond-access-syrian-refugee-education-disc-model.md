Beyond Access: What Ensures Syrian Refugee Students' Learning and Retention?
Baneen Rizvi, Gamze Venable, Lauren Evans, Safa' Obeid
Harvard Graduate School of Education
BA855: Education and International Development: Programming and Practice
Dr. Robert Jenkins
December 5, 2025

Abstract
Syrian refugee children of primary school age in Türkiye and Jordan are now largely integrated into national education systems, yet major gaps in learning and retention persist, closely tied to limited teacher capacity. This paper analyzes teacher professional development as the central lever for improving learning and retention outcomes, documents the scope of PD deficits through original cross-national survey data, and proposes the DISC model — Diagnose, Implement, Sustain, Credit — a dual-track teacher development framework for both host-country and refugee teachers.

The Problem
Inclusion policies have prioritized structural integration — placing refugee learners in national schools under host-country curricula — over improving the quality of teaching they receive. In both Türkiye and Jordan, teachers working with Syrian students frequently report feeling underprepared to manage large, multi-level classes of learners with complex psychosocial needs.

Scale of the challenge:

In 2021, Türkiye hosted 1.28 million school-age refugee children
Jordan hosted 260,000 school-age refugee children
Teachers report lacking preparation in psychological support, second-language instruction, learning gap management, and multi-level classroom management
Original Survey Data
Survey: 55 teachers of Syrian refugee students in Türkiye (n=22) and Jordan (n=33) via multilingual online questionnaire. 53% had more than ten years of experience.

Key findings:

80% of teachers (44/55) reported receiving no specialized training to support refugee learners
Türkiye: 64% received no specialized training
Jordan: 91% received no specialized training
Only 4% received training on educational transition
Only 13% received training on inclusive or differentiated instruction
Zero teachers in Türkiye received second-language acquisition training
The DISC Model
A dual-track professional development framework providing holistic support to both host-country and refugee teachers, drawing on lessons from displacement-affected contexts.

Stage 1: Diagnose
Inspired by the World Bank's RAPID framework. Baseline assessment of skills and needs of both host-country and refugee teachers.

Host-country teachers: Diagnostic tools focus on competencies in language support, trauma awareness and psychosocial response, differentiated instruction, and inclusive classroom practices.

Refugee teachers: Diagnosis focuses on validating existing qualifications, assessing skill level, and evaluating mental well-being and teaching readiness.

Stage 2: Implement
Tailored PD packages for each track based on Diagnose findings.

Host-country teachers — Jordan: Four-part PD package — second-language awareness, trauma-informed pedagogy, differentiated instruction, and inclusive classroom practices.

Host-country teachers — Türkiye: Managing multi-level, linguistically diverse classes and supporting students with substantial learning gaps.

Refugee teachers: Training in host-country language, national curriculum, and alignment with host-country pedagogical norms.

Stage 3: Sustain
Moves away from one-off workshops toward school- and cluster-based professional learning communities and teacher support groups.

Host-country teachers: School/cluster PLCs and teacher support groups, drawing on promising experiences in Kenya.

Refugee teachers: Clear long-term integration pathway — initially employed as teaching assistants, progressively recognized with expanded qualifications and responsibilities.

Stage 4: Credit
Links teacher participation in PD to recognition, incentives, and opportunities for advancement.

Host-country teachers: Salary-scale progression, formal acknowledgements, and pathways to advanced certification — modeled on evidence from Kakuma refugee-camp schools in Kenya.

Refugee teachers: Recognizing prior qualifications through degree-equivalency frameworks, paid training pathways, and certification programs strengthening professional identity.

DISC Roll-out in Jordan
Stage	Timeline	Host-Country Teachers	Refugee Teachers
Diagnose	Months 1–2	Needs assessment via observations, surveys, interviews	Profile qualifications; check readiness
Implement	Months 1–12	PD on language support, trauma, differentiation, inclusion	Host-language and curriculum courses; mentored co-teaching
Sustain	Year 2+	Monthly PLCs; peer observation; coaching	Ongoing mentoring; expanded roles (TA → co-teacher)
Credit	Year 1–3	Micro-credentials; use in appraisal/promotion	Micro-credentials; bridges to certification
Stakeholders and Coordination
Effective implementation requires a coordinated multi-actor system:

Ministries of Education: Lead policy adoption
International organizations (UNICEF, UNHCR, World Bank): Co-develop diagnostic tools, PD content, digital platforms, monitoring systems
Local teacher-training institutions: Sustain the model by embedding refugee-education competencies in curricula
Financing: Domestic, multilateral, and bilateral funding with planned transition toward full national financing
Key Risk
Weak institutionalization: without strong coalitions among ministries, teacher unions, refugee communities, and international partners, shifting political priorities could undermine implementation. DISC must be embedded in durable institutional frameworks that can withstand political change.

Conclusion
Despite expanded access to schools in Türkiye and Jordan, Syrian refugee students still face learning and retention gaps. Strengthening teacher PD is a central, actionable lever. The DISC model — Diagnose, Implement, Sustain, Credit — transforms refugee education from access to sustained quality learning by integrating context-specific PD with continuous support and clear recognition.

Early Interventions for Children With Developmental Disabilities: Global Causal Evidence and Implications for Türkiye
Gamze Venable
Harvard Graduate School of Education
BA840: Using Evidence to Improve Teaching and Learning
Dr. Emiliana Vegas, Dr. Marty West
March 31, 2026

Overview
This systematic evidence review was conducted for Türkiye's Ministry of National Education (MoNE) to identify which early interventions (ages 0–5) most effectively improve outcomes for children with developmental delays and disabilities (DD/D). Despite legislative provisions since the 1980s, Türkiye lacks a coordinated early intervention system, standardized assessments, and sufficient trained personnel.

Research Question
Which early interventions for children ages 0–5 with developmental delays or disabilities produce the strongest causal evidence of improved educational outcomes, and what are the implications for Türkiye's Ministry of National Education?

Methods
Systematic search across ERIC, PubMed, Elicit, Google Scholar, HOLLIS, 3ie, World Bank Open Knowledge Repository, WWC, and EEF. Coverage: 2000–present, English and Turkish. Population: children prior to kindergarten entry (≤71 months) with diagnosed DD/D or eligible for early intervention or early childhood special education.

Key Findings
Three Studies Met Eligibility Criteria
Karaaslan et al. (2013) — Turkey (UMIC) — RCT

Responsive Teaching added to standard services over six months
Children receiving RT showed 42% improvement in developmental quotient vs. 7% in control group
Strongest causal warrant: randomly assigned, fidelity monitored
Language and personal-social domains also improved
Stingone et al. (2026) — USA (HIC) — Non-randomized, statistical matching

EI recipients performed slightly better in Grade 3 ELA (+0.045 SD)
Stronger effects for children later needing special education (ELA ratio 1.28 vs. 1.09 overall)
Key implication for MoNE: higher returns for children with greater developmental need
Sullivan & Field (2013) — USA (HIC) — Non-randomized, statistical weighting

Lower kindergarten reading (−0.21 SD) and math (−0.29 SD) among service recipients
Most likely reflects greater baseline need among recipients, not that services caused harm
Implications for MoNE
Defensible now:

Parent-mediated Responsive Teaching coaching can improve short-term developmental outcomes for children with DD/D in Türkiye under trial conditions
US evidence suggests receiving EI before age 3 is associated with small positive Grade 3 outcomes, with stronger signals for higher-need children
Not defensible yet:

Naming a single best model to sustain K–5 gains in Türkiye
Assuming early gains will persist into later academic outcomes
Applying US effect sizes to the Turkish context
Actionable next steps:

Prioritize expanding RT and similar parent-mediated programs with outcome evaluation built into implementation
Link early service records to later school data
Provide continued support through the transition into formal schooling to preserve early gains
Limitations
Three studies met eligibility criteria. Findings cannot be generalized. No Turkish-language causal studies were identified beyond Karaaslan et al. (2013). No formal risk-of-bias tool was applied.

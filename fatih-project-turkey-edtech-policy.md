Digital Transformation in Turkish Education: The FATİH Project
Gamze Venable
Harvard Graduate School of Education
EDU BA800: Introduction to International Education: Policy, Practice, and Community
Professor Fernando M. Reimers and Professor Robert Jenkins
September 1, 2025

Abstract
In 2010, Türkiye launched the FATİH Project — one of the world's most ambitious national educational technology initiatives. This $3 billion program aimed to digitally transform Türkiye's entire public education system by providing tablets, interactive whiteboards, and internet connectivity to over 40,000 schools, reaching 17 million students and one million teachers. This paper examines the FATİH Project's implementation, outcomes, and ongoing challenges over more than a decade of operation, drawing on official government reports and academic research.

Policy Relevance
The FATİH Project is a critical case study for understanding how large-scale AI and education technology initiatives can deepen rather than close educational inequity when deployed without equity-centered pedagogical design. Its lessons are directly applicable to current debates about AI deployment in education systems across low- and middle-income contexts.

Background
The FATİH Project emerged from Türkiye's broader national development strategy and educational reform agenda. Key goals:

Provide tablets, interactive whiteboards, and internet connectivity to 40,000+ public schools
Reach 17 million students and 1 million teachers
Reduce digital inequalities across Türkiye's diverse socioeconomic landscape
Total investment: approximately $3 billion USD
Four Critical Implementation Challenges
1. Infrastructure Deployment and Connectivity Inequity
What happened: Many schools — particularly in rural and underdeveloped regions — struggled with insufficient bandwidth, unstable electricity supply, and inadequate technical support.

Evidence: While interactive whiteboards were widely installed, internet access was inconsistent, limiting the use of digital resources (Kurt et al., 2013; Pamuk et al., 2013).

Impact: Technology provision without reliable infrastructure reduced effectiveness and reinforced rather than narrowed digital inequalities.

2. Teacher Professional Development — Pedagogical vs. Technical Training
What happened: Teachers were inadequately prepared to incorporate digital technologies into instructional practice. Professional development was limited to short-term technical training rather than continuous, pedagogically oriented programs.

Evidence: Research highlighted that PD focused on how to operate devices rather than how to teach with them (Akkoyunlu & Yılmaz-Soylu, 2015).

Impact: Teachers used digital devices in traditional, lecture-based ways rather than for interactive or student-centered learning. The transformative potential of the technology was not realized.

3. Digital Content Development — Hardware Before Software
What happened: High-quality, curriculum-aligned e-content lagged significantly behind hardware deployment. Teachers lacked adequate digital teaching materials.

Evidence: The Ministry of Education reported delays in producing and distributing digital content, leaving many teachers reliant on conventional textbooks (MEB, 2019).

Impact: Without engaging, localized educational content, devices were underutilized. Hardware without content is infrastructure without purpose.

4. Sustainability and Maintenance
What happened: Many tablets quickly became obsolete or were not replaced. Interactive whiteboards faced technical breakdowns without sufficient IT support.

Evidence: Academic evaluations noted the absence of a sustainable maintenance model (Selwyn, 2016; Türel & Johnson, 2012).

Impact: The lack of a sustainable maintenance model undermined continued use and long-term effectiveness.

Central Analytical Finding
The FATİH Project reveals a fundamental flaw in large-scale EdTech initiatives: prioritizing measurable outputs — device distribution — over complex pedagogical transformation processes that determine actual impact.

Technology can facilitate educational innovation. Its effectiveness hinges on:

Thoughtful integration into instructional practice
Robust, pedagogically focused teacher preparation
Alignment with educational aims — not technological advancement alone
Equity-centered design from the outset
Implications for AI in Education Policy
The FATİH Project's mixed outcomes are directly relevant to current AI deployment in education:

Equity must be designed in — not added later. Technology deployed without attention to who it reaches and how it is used will deepen existing inequalities.

Teacher capacity is the critical variable. No technology initiative succeeds without sustained investment in how teachers are prepared to use it.

Scale without fidelity produces harm. A $3 billion initiative can fail to improve learning outcomes when implementation quality is not prioritized alongside distribution.

Context determines impact. What works in high-income, high-infrastructure settings does not automatically transfer to low- and middle-income contexts.

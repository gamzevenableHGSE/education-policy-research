Building Critical Thinking for Nepal's First Liberal Arts University
Sophia Lee, Jayson Phillips, Baneen Rizvi, Gamze Venable
Published in: Leading Education Systems: Learning from Policy Challenges and Practices Around the World
Edited by Professor Fernando M. Reimers
Harvard Graduate School of Education, 2026
ISBN: 979-8252183442

Abstract
This chapter examines the critical thinking (CT) readiness gap facing the University of Nepal (UNepal) as it launches the country's first liberal arts university. UNepal seeks to cultivate critically engaged, creative, and civic-minded graduates through an interdisciplinary core curriculum, yet it will recruit students from a pre-tertiary system that remains heavily exam-oriented and teacher-centered, with limited opportunities for inquiry-based learning and argumentation.

Drawing on national assessment data and interviews with five senior UNepal leaders, this chapter analyzes root causes of this gap and evaluates five policy alternatives against weighted criteria derived from stakeholder input. The analysis identifies a required first-year, curriculum-embedded CT course as the most appropriate starting point for building a sustainable CT learning ecosystem at UNepal.

The Problem
UNepal's 2026 launch puts urgent pressure on an unresolved readiness gap. The new curriculum assumes students can interpret texts, assess evidence, construct arguments, and revise their beliefs in light of better reasons. Yet most applicants will come from a rigid, exam-oriented school system that rewards memorization and compliance over judgment and inquiry.

National data illustrate the scale:

SEE pass rates have averaged just 45.5% over the past two decades
More than half of students perform only at "pre-basic" or "basic" levels in mathematics and English
Research on Nepal's higher education sector reports limited CT dispositions among undergraduates
Root Causes
1. Exam-driven pedagogy
Classrooms remain dominated by chalk-and-talk instruction and high-stakes assessments that reward memorization over reasoning. When schooling systematically rewards compliance, students become reluctant to question authority and less habituated to sustained reasoning under uncertainty.

2. Resource and infrastructure inequity
A longstanding divide separates well-resourced urban private schools from under-resourced rural public schools. Large classes, limited resources, and restricted digital access limit teachers' ability to adopt collaborative and inquiry-based pedagogy.

3. Ineffective teacher professional development
PD programs are often designed by external experts and do not address localized classroom realities. Teachers frequently struggle to translate training into practice.

Five Policy Alternatives Evaluated
Alternative 1: Curriculum-Embedded CT Course
A mandatory first-year CT course designed around Nepal-grounded decision cases, structured dialogue and argument practice, and a draft-feedback-revision cycle.

Alternative 2: Summer Bridge Program
Pre-enrollment modules in writing, oral expression, problem-solving, and quantitative reasoning for underprepared students. Regional comparator: Fulbright University Vietnam's seven-week bridge program.

Alternative 3: Faculty Development Framework
Foundational pedagogical capacity-building through faculty learning communities, demand-driven global knowledge partnerships, and community-based curricular co-creation.

Alternative 4: Institution-Wide CT Assessment Framework
Faculty-led CT assessment using adapted VALUE rubrics with calibration sessions, plus standardized CT skills and disposition tools administered early and later in the program.

Alternative 5: All-in-One CT Ecosystem
Long-term integrated model combining all four components. Functions as an aspirational end state rather than a 2026 launch option.

Recommended Sequencing
Year 1: Faculty development and CT assessment framework — institutionalize expectations
Year 1–2: Mandatory first-year CT course — deliver immediate readiness impact
Year 2+: Bridge program elements for most underprepared students
Long term: Move toward all-in-one ecosystem as capacity grows
Key Risks
Uneven faculty participation in PD or assessment calibration
Leadership turnover shifting priorities
Budget constraints limiting scale and continuity
Fee-based bridge programs reproducing inequity — mitigated through targeted subsidies
Broader Implications
Although grounded in the specific case of UNepal, this analysis highlights principles for liberal arts institutions in low- and middle-income settings. Treating CT as a core organizing competency rather than an elective add-on, and aligning curriculum, assessment, and faculty development around that competency, can help move CT from rhetoric to institutional practice.

Methodological Note
Criterion weights were derived from percentage allocations provided by five senior UNepal leaders through structured interviews conducted December 1–3, 2025. Each alternative was rated on a 1–5 scale for each criterion by the authors and averaged to produce the policy matrix.

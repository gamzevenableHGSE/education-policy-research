"We Will Live Here": Structures, Teachers, and the Permanent Lives of Syrian Children in Türkiye
Gamze Venable
Harvard Graduate School of Education
2026

Abstract
The Syrian civil war displaced millions of families, many of whom settled permanently in Türkiye — the country that now hosts more registered Syrian refugees than any other in the world (UNHCR, 2024). Today, a generation of children born in Türkiye to Syrian parents attends Turkish public schools under temporary protection status. Their identification numbers begin with 99, a bureaucratic notation designed to mark non-permanence, regardless of where they were born or what lives they have built.

This paper analyzes how structural violence (Galtung, 1969) is perpetuated not through acts of hostility but through the ordinary administration of educational systems designed for a crisis that has become a permanent condition. Drawing on a sixty-minute qualitative interview with Gül — a pseudonym for a Turkish public elementary school teacher with seventeen years of experience — the analysis examines nine dimensions of how classification systems, language policy, resource allocation, and institutional design shape the educational experiences of Syrian-born children in Türkiye.

The central finding is that education policy in Türkiye has not caught up to the permanent lives Syrian-born children are already living. Structural reform — not individual teacher generosity — is what this generation requires.

Theoretical Frameworks
Structural Violence (Galtung, 1969)
Harm embedded in social structures that denies human potential without a single identifiable perpetrator. Applied here to examine how educational systems reproduce the effects of displacement through ordinary institutional practice.

Desire-Based Framework (Tuck, 2009)
Concerned with the lifeways and longings of communities — not just the damages done to them. Applied here to document what teachers, children, and families are already building within structural constraints: belonging, connection, and futures the legal system has not yet found a way to recognize.

Research Design
Sixty-minute qualitative interview with Gül — a Turkish public elementary school teacher with seventeen years of experience, four of which have been spent with her current cohort in Grades 1–4. Interview conducted in Turkish, translated by the researcher. Analysis organized around nine dimensions of structural and relational experience.

Nine Dimensions of Analysis
1. Who Counts: Classification and the Architecture of Access
EU-funded programs, material kits, and adaptation classes are available only to students administratively classified as refugees. Origin, not need, determines access. Classification functions as a resource gate before a teacher has spoken a single word to the child it marks.

2. The Chronosystem Has Shifted: Syrian Students Are Not Newcomers
Most current Syrian students were born in Türkiye. Gül's Syrian student arrived at approximately age one or two. His Turkish was functional from the beginning of schooling. The institutional framework — designed for newly arriving children facing an acute newcomer emergency — has not caught up to this reality.

3. The Adaptation Class: A Program That Has Outlived Its Premise
The adaptation class was designed for newly arriving children entering Turkish schools with no prior language exposure. By March 2024, EUR 2.37 billion had been allocated for education in Türkiye, with PIKTES alone valued at EUR 997 million. The program trained 93,124 staff — yet Gül cannot recall receiving specific in-service training on refugee integration. Large-scale programs do not automatically reach the classrooms where children sit.

4. Language Policy in Practice: Enforcement, Desire, and the Space Between
Turkish-only is actively enforced: warnings to children, phone calls to families, instructions to peers not to assist in home languages. The Turkish-only norm enacts structural violence through ordinary practice — a systematic denial of linguistic identity through which a child thinks, knows, and belongs. Yet 90% of children acquire Turkish through the desire to play and connect — not through enforcement.

5. Cultural Flexibility: Partial Moves Toward Belonging
Gül exempts non-Turkish-origin students from national anthem memorization. Most decline the exemption: "We will memorize it, teacher." Families choose participation beyond what institutions require. Children are already reaching toward belonging.

6. The Teacher as the Critical Variable: Relational Practice and Its Structural Limits
Teacher attitude is the most influential factor in Syrian students' classroom experience. Gül's relational investment — four years of continuous relationship — has protected her student from reported experiences of exclusion. But a system that depends on individual teacher commitment will fail wherever a teacher is indifferent, overburdened, or less fortunate in assignment.

7. Material Solidarity Among Teachers: Quiet Equity Practice
Gül describes an informal teacher network that activates when formal systems fall short — desks, clothing, shoes. This support is real, immediate, consequential, and entirely invisible to formal institutional records. Where PIKTES has allocated EUR 997 million, a teacher is still calling colleagues to ask if anyone has a spare desk.

8. "We Will Live Here": The Permanence Paradox
A 9-year-old boy explains: "Teacher, we have no relatives left there anymore. Our village is not like it used to be. All the people we know are here, and since we have no one left there, we will live here." This child was born in Türkiye. He has never lived in Syria. Turkish law classifies him under temporary protection status. His identification number begins with 99 — marking non-permanence regardless of the life he has built. As of April 2023, the supply of 99-prefixed numbers was exhausted; the system began issuing 98-prefixed numbers. Holders of these numbers are ineligible to vote under the Turkish constitution.

9. Toward Educational Equity: Synthesis
Two dimensions must be held together: the reproduction of structural inequity through ordinary institutional practice, and the active challenge to those inequities by teachers, families, and children building belonging without institutional permission. By March 2024, over 200,000 refugee children remained out of education in Türkiye even after EUR 2.37 billion in EU investment. The structural ceiling is real and documented.

Central Finding
Education policy in Türkiye has not caught up to the permanent lives Syrian-born children are already living. The 99-identification number, the adaptation class, and the Turkish-only language norm were each designed for a newcomer emergency. They now govern the lives of children who have never lived anywhere else.

What Gül's account ultimately requires is a system that catches up to the reality its children are already living:

Legal recognition of the permanence Syrian children have built — not as a future aspiration but as a present fact
Institutional support designed for who these children actually are, not who emergency frameworks assumed they would be
Teacher training that equips educators to examine the structural conditions they enact, not only the relational ones they choose
Structures that do not leave equity dependent on the fortune of landing in Gül's classroom
Methodological Note
This narrative was constructed from Gül's memory and filtered through researcher interpretation. The Syrian student at its center is its subject, not its narrator. His voice reaches this analysis only once — in the sentence that opens and closes it. Interview conducted in Turkish, translated by the researcher.
